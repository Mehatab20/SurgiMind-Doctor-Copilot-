TITLE: Ventricular Fibrillation Arrest and Cardiac Arrest – Perioperative Surgical Protocol

CATEGORY: Cardiology | Emergency | Critical Care | Resuscitation

OVERVIEW:
Cardiac arrest is the abrupt cessation of effective cardiac output resulting in global tissue ischaemia. Ventricular fibrillation (VF) is the most common initial rhythm in out-of-hospital cardiac arrest and is immediately life-threatening without defibrillation. Surgical intervention following cardiac arrest is reserved for reversible underlying causes, specifically obstructive or anatomical pathology, and carries extreme perioperative risk requiring intensive multidisciplinary management.

SURGICAL INDICATIONS:
- Cardiac tamponade causing arrest: emergency pericardiocentesis or pericardial window
- Tension pneumothorax causing arrest: immediate needle decompression then thoracostomy
- Massive pulmonary embolism with arrest: surgical embolectomy or catheter-directed thrombolysis
- Aortic dissection type A causing cardiac arrest: emergent cardiothoracic surgery
- Post-cardiac arrest with identified surgically correctable lesion (e.g. ruptured AAA, perforated viscus)
- Refractory VF arrest in cath lab: consider extracorporeal CPR (eCPR) bridge to CABG
- Traumatic cardiac arrest with penetrating thoracic injury: emergency department thoracotomy

CONTRAINDICATIONS FOR ELECTIVE SURGERY:
- Active or recent (< 6 months) cardiac arrest without full neurological and cardiac recovery
- Unresolved post-arrest cardiogenic shock requiring vasopressor support
- Post-arrest therapeutic hypothermia phase still active (first 24 hours)
- Severe neurological impairment following hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy (CPC score 3-4)
- Haemodynamic instability: MAP < 65 mmHg despite optimised vasopressor therapy
- Active ventricular arrhythmias not controlled on antiarrhythmic therapy
- EF < 20% without cardiac optimisation or mechanical circulatory support (MCS) plan
- Unresolved metabolic derangements: pH < 7.2, lactate > 6 mmol/L, K+ > 6.0 mEq/L

PRE-OPERATIVE CHECKLIST:
1. 12-lead ECG and continuous cardiac monitoring immediately post-ROSC
2. Arterial blood gas: pH, pCO2, pO2, lactate, bicarbonate (serial every 2 hours)
3. Troponin I/T at presentation and 3-6 hours post-arrest (STEMI equivalent: emergent cath lab)
4. Echocardiography (TTE/TOE): assess wall motion, tamponade, EF, structural abnormalities
5. Full blood count: haemoglobin, haematocrit, platelets (post-CPR haematological shifts)
6. Coagulation panel: PT, PTT, INR, fibrinogen, D-dimer (post-arrest coagulopathy common)
7. Comprehensive metabolic panel: electrolytes, renal function, glucose, LFTs
8. Chest X-ray: pneumothorax, pulmonary oedema, ETT and line placement
9. CT aorta / CT pulmonary angiogram if dissection or PE suspected as precipitant
10. Targeted temperature management (TTM) protocol 32-36°C for 24 hours if comatose post-ROSC
11. Emergent cardiac catheterisation if STEMI identified on post-ROSC ECG
12. Neurology review: EEG if persistent coma post-arrest, assess for post-anoxic seizures
13. Anaesthesiology early involvement for surgical planning post-stabilisation
14. ICU admission mandatory: invasive arterial monitoring, central venous access, Foley catheter
15. POLST/ACP review and family goals-of-care discussion before any elective procedure

RED FLAGS / ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- Return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) not achieved: survival for surgical intervention requires ROSC
- Ongoing CPR without ROSC > 30 minutes (non-shockable rhythm) in non-hypothermic patient: termination of resuscitation consideration
- pH < 6.9 with no reversible aetiology identified: extreme perioperative mortality
- Serum lactate > 15 mmol/L post-resuscitation: near-universal mortality in non-surgical patients
- Active VF or pulseless VT: operating during active arrest only in ED thoracotomy scenarios
- Bilateral fixed dilated pupils > 4 hours post-ROSC in normothermic patient: poor neurological prognosis
- Severe post-arrest ARDS (PaO2/FiO2 < 100): ventilatory compromise precludes safe intubation for elective surgery
- Bowel ischaemia / mesenteric infarction following prolonged arrest: extremely high operative mortality
- No surgical decision should proceed without neurology prognosis clarification post-TTM
